About

A Community Built
on Connection

McMurtry College opened in fall 2009 as Rice University's tenth residential college. Designed by the acclaimed Hopkins Architects, the building achieved LEED Gold certification, setting a new standard for sustainable campus living.

Named for Burt and Deedee McMurtry, Rice Class of 1956, the college features a distinctive horseshoe-shaped plan surrounding a central courtyard. Rising five stories, McMurtry offers residents a campfire pit, rooftop terrace with sweeping Houston skyline views, kitchens on every level, a teahouse, game room, music room, gym, and the only circular commons on campus.

Architecture

Hopkins Architects

Drawing inspiration from Rice's signature Italian Romanesque style, adapted for the Texas coastal plain. The brick facades echo the rich character of historic campus structures, while thick exterior walls provide high thermal mass. Shaded arcades and covered walkways weave seamlessly with existing tree-lined paths.

Sustainable Design

Double-brick walls with high thermal mass reduce energy consumption. Green roofs and native landscaping minimize environmental impact.

Collegiate Quadrangle

A horseshoe-shaped plan creates an intimate central courtyard, continuing Rice's tradition of inward-facing residential communities.

Connected Campus

Covered walkways and shaded arcades integrate naturally with the original Cram, Goodhue & Ferguson campus plan.

The Commons

The Yurt

McMurtry's commons carries its nickname with pride. When Hurricane Ike delayed construction in 2009, the college gathered in a temporary tent. That spirit of resilience gave the circular commons its name. Today, the Yurt stands as the only round commons on Rice's campus, serving as the heart of McMurtry's social life.

The commons hosts weekly college nights, study breaks, and the traditions that bind each class of McMurtrians together.

Our Namesakes

Burt & Deedee McMurtry

Rice Class of 1956

Burt McMurtry studied electrical engineering at Rice before earning his master's at Stanford. He went on to become one of Silicon Valley's most respected venture capitalists, backing companies that would reshape the technology landscape, including Microsoft and Compaq. He served seventeen years on Rice University's Board of Governors.

Together with Deedee, who volunteers with the American Conservatory Theater, the McMurtrys established endowed professorships and scholarships at Rice. Their generosity made possible the college that now bears their name, a living testament to their belief in the transformative power of residential education.
